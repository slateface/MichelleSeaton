Flu Season

Wilamette Week -Johanna Droubay

In the decaying psychiatric ward of Theatre vertigo’s The Flu Season, sheetrock rots away from the structure of a wall, medical gauze hangs from the ceiling like damaged cobwebs, and tow young crazies fall madly in love. But that’s only half of it.

In Act 2 lover boy (Mario Calcagno) experiences a change of heart that unhinges his female friend (the deliciously subtle Heather Rose Walters). As a result, the ruin predicted by the set design comes miserably to pass.

That night WW saw the play, the audience knew Vertigo company members Todd Van Voris and Darius Pierce so well that upon their unannounced arrival before the show’s started silence filled the house. The actors-dressed in what could be considered street cloths-discreetly took their seats reserved for them and sat with a sizeable audience in absolute silence, house lights all ablaze. It was an eerie experience: the oft-employed trick of embedding the actors among their spectators didn’t work here-and it shouldn’t. That’s because throughout playwright Will Eno’s comedic drama nothing quite passes for real.

No tender love scenes nor heated argument goes on without interruptions from Prologue (Van Voris) and Epilogue (Pierce), constant reminders that the action before us is “only” theater. Moreover, there is no gradual awakening to the fact that the psychiatrists in this hospital are as mad as their patients-that much is clear from the get go.

Doctor and patient’s mutual insanity is Eno’s launching pad, not his point finale. Consequently, the Flu Season is no mere sendup of mental health care. It’s a sickness, not a satire. And Theatre Vertigo’s strain of the virus is characterized by hot flashes of insight, chilling honesty and loads of lightheaded hilarity.
