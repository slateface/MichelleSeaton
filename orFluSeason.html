The Flu Season...

The Oregonian, -Richard Wattenberg

"Theatre Vertigo launches its season with a polished, if not entirely flawless, production of contemporary playwright Will Eno’s knotty little piece “The Flu Season.” Eno offers us a postmodern love story, which is to say that this is a story about a love story, a play about writing a play. While it follows the ups and downs of a brief romance between the Man and the Woman, two residents in a mental health institution, the real tension reside in and between the two characters, Prologue and Epilogue, who embody the `author’s` response to his own story. Through them we see the “author’s” haunting suspicion that life lacks the order and inherent purposefulness that he wants to write into his tale, instead things just happen.

The first act is full of a kind of absurdist humor. Man, Woman, their Doctor and their Nurse engage in straggling efforts to communicate. Each of the four follows his/her own eccentric line of thought, and only occasionally do these lines intersect. By act’s end, however, the Man and Woman become involved. Their affair quickly turns sour in Act II. For no apparent reason, the Man once again goes off on his own tangent, and tragedy ensues. There is room for sadness here, but the production fails too far into an elegiac tone to convey the depths of Prologue and Epilogue’s twisted anguish. Sentiment overwhelms the play’s probing examination of out futile efforts to give form to life. As with other Theatre Vertigo productions, the acting ensemble, directed by Michelle Seaton, is strong. While both Todd Van Voris and Darius Pierce, who play Prologue and Epilogue, can be rather mannered, they use theatricality to good effort. As Man, Mario Calcagno has a magnetic energy even if the more gentle sensitivity that his character seems to manifest at play’s end seems a bit unmotivated. Though somewhat tentative early on, Heather Rose Walters offers a moving portrayal of the fragile, lonely Woman."
